# BRAND.md Quality Guide

> Annotated reference showing what separates a strong BRAND.md from a generic one. Each section shows a **good excerpt** (from a real brand book), a **bad counter-example**, and the **quality differentiator** to hit.
>
> **IMPORTANT: Match the STRUCTURAL QUALITY, not the specific content.** The good excerpts below are from an existing brand (a canvas/notes app with glass surfaces and amethyst palette). Your output will have completely different content — different product, different visual language, different archetype. What should be identical is the DEPTH: narrative prose for stories, specific triggers for emotional journeys, CSS-level formulas for materials, named animations with physics values. The excerpts illustrate the quality bar, not the answer.

---

## The Origin Story

**Quality differentiator:** Narrative prose with emotional stakes and cultural context. NOT bullet points, NOT a PRD summary.

**Good:**
> Conquistador is Spanish for "conqueror." Conquer the blank page. Conquer the grid. Conquer the conventional boundaries of how thought gets organized.
>
> The name is a declaration: thinking doesn't happen in rows and columns. It happens spatially. Ideas cluster, overlap, drift, connect across distances. Conquistador gives that mess a canvas and gets out of the way.

**Bad:**
> Conquistador is a note-taking app built in 2024. We founded it because existing tools were too rigid. Our goal is to help people organize their thoughts better.

**Why the good version works:**
- Opens with the name's meaning — immediate cultural anchor
- Uses active verbs tied to the product's core action ("conquer the blank page")
- Third paragraph explains the product philosophy, not just the feature set
- The reader feels the product's worldview, not just its function

## The Name

**Quality differentiator:** Etymology, cultural context, pronunciation, and 3-4 meanings tied to product/brand/experience. NOT "we named it X because it sounds good."

**Good:**
> **Primary reading:** "Con-kees-ta-dor." Spanish noun, from Latin "conquistare" (to conquer, to acquire).
>
> **Why a historical name, not a tech name:** A name rooted in exploration history signals that this tool has roots deeper than the latest productivity trend.
>
> **What "conquer" means for Conquistador:**
> - Conquer the blank page (infinite canvas, not empty documents)
> - Conquer the grid (spatial note placement, not rows)
> - Conquer isolation (real-time collaboration)
> - Conquer the paywall barrier (generous free tier)

**Bad:**
> We chose the name "Conquistador" because it sounds powerful and memorable. It reflects our mission to help users conquer their work.

**Why the good version works:**
- Pronunciation guide for non-obvious names
- Etymological depth (Latin root)
- Justifies why THIS type of name, not just why this name
- Each "meaning" maps to a specific product capability, not abstract aspirations

## Core Values

**Quality differentiator:** "X over Y" format where Y is a legitimate alternative. 2-3 sentences explaining what the value means in practice — specific behavioral standards.

**Good:**
> **Craft over features.** We ship fewer things, but every interaction feels considered. We will delay a release to get an animation curve right rather than ship a feature list. This means saying no to "power user" requests that would clutter the surface.

**Bad:**
> **Quality.** We believe in delivering the highest quality product to our users.

**Why the good version works:**
- "Craft over features" has a real tradeoff — features is a legitimate alternative
- Names a specific sacrifice ("delay a release to get an animation curve right")
- States what the value causes the team to say NO to
- The opposite value ("features over craft") is something real companies actually choose

## Personality Traits

**Quality differentiator:** Table with "What it means / What it doesn't mean" columns, product-specific examples in both columns.

**Good:**
> | **Refined, but not precious** | The UI uses glassmorphism and geometric typography because those choices serve clarity, not decoration. We never add visual flourish that slows someone down. | We don't treat the UI as a museum piece. If a glass effect hurts performance, it goes. |

**Bad:**
> | **Refined** | We are sophisticated and elegant | We are not sloppy |

**Why the good version works:**
- "But not precious" is the guardrail — prevents the trait from becoming its own extreme
- Both columns reference specific product decisions (glassmorphism, performance)
- A designer can make actual decisions from this row

## Emotional Journey Map

**Quality differentiator:** Touchpoint-by-touchpoint map (6-10 touchpoints) with specific emotions AND specific triggers. NOT just before/during/after.

**Good:**
> | **First encounter** | Intrigue | The name is unusual (Latin?). The geometric type stands out. The deep amethyst palette and glass surfaces aren't what you expect from a "notes app." |
> | **Hitting the free tier limit** | Respect | Clear message: you've reached 3 boards. Pro gives you unlimited. No guilt trip, no modal that blocks your work. |
> | **Telling someone** | Pride | "Try Conquistador. It's this canvas app with glass surfaces where you just... put sticky notes anywhere." |

**Bad:**
> | **Before** | Frustrated | Users are unhappy with current tools |
> | **During** | Happy | Users enjoy using the product |
> | **After** | Satisfied | Users feel good about their work |

**Why the good version works:**
- Each touchpoint is a specific moment in the product experience, not a vague phase
- Emotions are specific ("intrigue," "respect," "pride") not generic ("happy," "satisfied")
- Triggers name exact product elements, not abstract benefits
- The "hitting a limit" and "telling someone" touchpoints reveal brand character at friction points and advocacy moments — the most revealing brand tests

## Tone Range

**Quality differentiator:** 3 key contexts that define the range (marketing = expressive, product UI = neutral, errors = constrained). Each has a tone shift description AND a concrete example.

**Good:**
> Default dimensional position: Formal ←[X]→ Casual (30% casual), Serious ←---[X]→ Playful (40%), etc.
>
> | **Marketing / landing page** | Inviting + confident. Let the visuals speak. | "Break the page." |
> | **Product UI** | Minimal + warm. Functional but not cold. | "New note" not "Create a new note on this board." |
> | **Error / friction** | Calm + honest. What happened, what to do. | "Couldn't save that note. Retrying now. Your text is safe locally." |

**Bad:**
> | **Pricing page** | Professional | |
> | **Error messages** | Empathetic | |
> | **Social media** | Fun | |
> | [9 more rows with single-word descriptions and no examples...]

**Why the good version works:**
- 3 contexts define the full range — any surface can interpolate from these three
- Tone shift is described in two adjectives that CREATE tension (inviting + confident, calm + honest)
- Every row has a concrete copy example
- Doesn't overextend into copy production — 3 examples calibrate the voice, 9 examples IS copywriting

## Tagline

**Quality differentiator:** Primary tagline scored with V/F/U rubric. One tagline — platform variants belong in copywriting.

**Good:**
> **Primary:** "Break the page."
>
> V:3 F:2 U:3 = 8/9.
> - Visual: a page cracking open into infinite space — strong image
> - Falsifiable: most apps respect the page — this claims the opposite
> - Unique: tied to the infinite canvas concept — no competitor would say this

**Bad:**
> **Tagline:** "Think Better Together"

**Why the good version works:**
- Scored against a rubric (V/F/U) — prevents generic taglines from passing
- The competitor swap test: could Miro say "Think Better Together"? Yes. Could Miro say "Break the page"? No. That's the difference.
- One tagline in the brand system. Platform-specific variants (App Store subtitle, social bio) are produced by copywriting, which consumes the voice DNA from BRAND.md

## Product-Specific Brand Sections

**Quality differentiator:** Sections unique to THIS product that carry brand weight. Explains WHAT the element is AND WHY it reinforces positioning.

**Good:**
> ## Nature Theme as Brand Differentiator
>
> **1. Product differentiation:** No major canvas app ships an earth-tone theme with greenhouse glass surfaces.
> **2. Conversion driver:** Nature is a Pro feature ($5.99/month). The most visible upgrade.
> **3. Brand identity extension:** Connects the brand to the physical world: craft paper, olive ink, greenhouse glass.
> **4. Accessibility story:** Three themes means users choose the visual environment that works best.

**Bad:**
> ## Features
> We have a light mode and dark mode plus a special nature theme.

**Why the good version works:**
- A unique product feature is analyzed through 4 brand lenses (differentiation, conversion, identity, accessibility)
- Each point explains WHY this feature matters to the brand, not just WHAT it is
- This section couldn't exist in any other brand book — it's specific to this product

## Digital Touchpoints

**Quality differentiator:** Each touchpoint describes HOW the brand's visual, verbal, and interaction choices manifest at that specific surface.

**Good:**
> | **Landing page** | Full glass system. Layered translucent panels. Clash Display heroes. Floating glass-effect sticky notes. Three-theme preview. |
> | **Email** | Minimal. Clash Display for headlines (with Georgia fallback), General Sans for body (with system-ui fallback). |

**Bad:**
> | **Landing page** | Show product features |
> | **Email** | Send updates to users |

**Why the good version works:**
- Names specific design elements (glass system, Clash Display, three-theme preview)
- Even email gets typography specifications with fallback fonts
- A designer could build each touchpoint from this description alone
